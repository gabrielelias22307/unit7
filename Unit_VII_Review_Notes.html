7.1 The Industrial Revolution
▼

What was the Industrial Revolution?

A transformation from agrarian, handicraft economies to one dominated by industry and machine manufacturing — beginning in Great Britain in the mid-1700s and spreading globally.

Why Great Britain First?

  • Abundant coal and iron ore deposits
  • Navigable rivers and coastline for transportation
  • Vast colonial empire (raw materials + markets)
  • Stable government encouraging trade
  • Skilled labor force and entrepreneurial culture
  • Water power from rivers

Key Inventions & Changes

  • Steam engine (James Watt) — powered factories & transportation
  • Textile machinery — spinning jenny, power loom
  • Shift from cottage industry to factory system
  • Coal replaced wood as primary fuel
  • Fossil fuels drove industrialization
  • Railroads connected industrial regions

Spread of Industrialization

  • Western Europe (Ruhr Valley, Germany)
  • United States (Pittsburgh, Midwest)
  • Japan (late 19th century)
  • Later: Soviet Union, China
  • Spread along trade and colonial networks

Urbanization & Population

  • Industrialization drove rapid urbanization
  • Workers moved from rural farms to urban factories
  • Cities grew dramatically near industrial sites
  • Population boom: better food supply & medicine
  • Poor urban living conditions initially (tenements)

The Berlin Conference (1884–1885)

  • European powers divided Africa among themselves without African input
  • Goal: access to raw materials (rubber, ivory, minerals) and new markets
  • Fueled further industrial expansion in Europe
  • Led to colonialism and long-term economic inequality

7.2 Economic Sectors & Industrial Location
▼
Sector Description Examples
Primary Extraction of raw materials from the natural environment Farming, mining, fishing, forestry, ranching
Secondary Manufacturing and processing raw materials into finished goods Steel mills, auto factories, food processing
Tertiary Service industry — connecting producers to consumers Retail, banking, education, healthcare, tourism
Quaternary Knowledge-based services — research and information R&D, information technology, consulting, finance
Quinary Highest-level executive and government decision-making CEOs, government officials, top scientists, judges

Weber's Least Cost Theory

Alfred Weber (1909) argued that industries locate where production costs are minimized — particularly transportation, labor, and agglomeration economies.

  • Transportation costs — the primary factor; minimize distance between raw materials and markets
  • Labor costs — industries may relocate for cheaper labor even if transportation costs rise
  • Agglomeration — clustering of industries to share infrastructure and services
  • Material Index — ratio of weight of inputs to weight of outputs; >1 means locate near raw materials; <1 means locate near markets
  • Weight-losing industries (e.g., copper smelting) locate near raw materials
  • Weight-gaining industries (e.g., bottling) locate near consumers

Break-of-Bulk Point

  • A location where goods are transferred from one mode of transportation to another
  • Typically located at ports, airports, or rail junctions
  • Industries often locate here to reduce shipping costs
  • Example: goods arriving by ship, transferred to trucks

Core-Periphery Model

  • Core: wealthy, industrialized regions that dominate the economy
  • Periphery: less developed regions supplying raw materials
  • Resources and wealth flow from periphery to core
  • Relates to Wallerstein's World System Theory (see 7.5)

7.3 Measures of Development
▼

Economic Indicators

  • GDP (Gross Domestic Product) — total value of goods & services produced within a country's borders
  • GNP (Gross National Product) — total value produced by a country's nationals, including abroad
  • GNI per capita (Gross National Income) — GNI divided by population; World Bank's preferred measure
  • Higher GNI per capita generally indicates greater development
  • Limitations: doesn't capture inequality or quality of life

Social Indicators

  • Literacy rate — % of adults who can read/write; higher = more developed
  • Infant mortality rate — deaths per 1,000 live births; lower = more developed
  • Access to health care — doctors per capita, hospitals, life expectancy
  • Fertility rate — higher in developing countries; linked to education & economic opportunity
  • Life expectancy — longer in more developed countries

Human Development Index (HDI)

A composite measure created by the UN that combines three dimensions of development:

  • Life expectancy — a long and healthy life
  • Education — years of schooling and expected years of schooling
  • Standard of living — GNI per capita (PPP)

HDI score ranges from 0 to 1; higher scores = greater development. Countries are ranked as very high, high, medium, or low development.

Gender Inequality Index (GII)

  • Measures gender-based disadvantages in three dimensions: reproductive health, empowerment, labor market
  • Higher GII score = greater inequality between genders
  • Often higher in less developed countries

Formal vs. Informal Economy

  • Formal economy — official, taxed, regulated, counted in GDP
  • Informal economy — unofficial, untaxed, unregulated (street vendors, day labor)
  • Informal sector is larger in developing countries
  • Workers have fewer protections in informal economy
Key Insight: No single indicator fully captures development. The HDI combines economic and social measures, but even it has limitations — it doesn't capture inequality within a country (addressed by the Inequality-adjusted HDI).

7.4 Women & Economic Development
▼

Gender Wage Gap

  • Women earn less than men in virtually every country
  • Gap is larger in developing countries
  • Caused by: discrimination, occupational segregation, unpaid domestic labor
  • Reducing the gap is linked to economic growth
  • GEM (Gender Empowerment Measure) tracks women's participation in political & economic life

Employment Opportunities

  • Women's employment rates vary widely by region and culture
  • Higher education for women → higher labor force participation
  • Women often concentrated in low-paying service jobs
  • Export-processing zones often employ large numbers of women
  • When women work, families and economies benefit

Microloans

Small loans (sometimes just a few dollars) given to poor entrepreneurs — especially women — in developing countries to start or expand small businesses.

  • Pioneered by Muhammad Yunus and the Grameen Bank in Bangladesh
  • Allow women to buy equipment, stock, or materials without access to traditional banks
  • Recipients often lack collateral but repayment rates are very high
  • Creates a multiplier effect: one loan → business → income → school fees → community growth
  • Muhammad Yunus won the Nobel Peace Prize (2006) for this innovation
Big Picture: When women are educated, employed, and economically empowered, countries develop faster. Investing in women's economic participation is one of the most effective development strategies.

3What factors can be used to measure gender inequality? How do these measures differ between developed and developing countries?
4Why is women's economic empowerment considered a key driver of national development? Provide specific examples.
7.5 Theories of Development
▼

Rostow's Stages of Economic Growth (Modernization Theory)

1
Traditional Society
Subsistence farming; limited technology; rigid social structure
2
Preconditions for Take-off
External investment; entrepreneurship emerges; infrastructure develops
3
Take-off
Rapid industrial growth; urbanization; manufacturing expands
4
Drive to Maturity
Diversified economy; technology spreads; global trade integration
5
Age of High Mass Consumption
Consumer goods dominate; service sector leads; high living standards
Critique of Rostow: Assumes all countries follow the same path as Western nations. Critics argue it ignores colonial history and global power inequalities that prevent developing countries from advancing freely.
Core
Core Countries
  • Highly developed
  • High wages & technology
  • Dominate global trade
  • USA, W. Europe, Japan
Semi-Periphery
Semi-Periphery
  • Industrializing nations
  • Some manufacturing
  • Between core & periphery
  • Brazil, China, India
Periphery
Periphery Countries
  • Less developed
  • Low wages
  • Export raw materials
  • Sub-Saharan Africa

Wallerstein's World System Theory

  • The capitalist world economy is divided into core, semi-periphery, and periphery
  • Core countries extract wealth from periphery
  • This system maintains global inequality
  • Countries can move between categories but the hierarchy persists

Dependency Theory

  • Underdevelopment is not a natural stage but a result of exploitation
  • Core countries deliberately keep periphery countries dependent
  • Trade relationships are unequal (low-value exports, high-value imports)
  • Commodity dependence: relying on one or few raw material exports

7.6 Trade & the World Economy
▼

Comparative Advantage

A country should specialize in producing goods it can make most efficiently relative to other goods — even if another country can make it cheaper in absolute terms. This specialization maximizes global production.

Complementary Advantage

Countries trade because they have different resources and specializations — what one country produces, another needs. This creates mutually beneficial trade relationships.

Trade Barriers

  • Tariff — a tax on imported goods; protects domestic industries but raises prices for consumers
  • Trade embargo — a complete ban on trade with a country (e.g., US embargo on Cuba)
  • Quotas — limits on the amount of a good that can be imported
  • Subsidies — government support for domestic industries to make them more competitive

Free Trade

  • Trade without tariffs, quotas, or other barriers
  • Increases efficiency and lowers prices
  • Critics: can hurt domestic workers and industries
  • Promoted by WTO, regional trade agreements

Major Trade Organizations & Agreements

  • WTO (World Trade Organization) — sets rules for international trade; resolves trade disputes between member countries
  • EU (European Union) — free trade bloc and political union; common market with shared currency (Euro) among many members
  • OPEC (Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries) — oil cartel that controls production to influence global oil prices
  • MERCOSUR — South American trade bloc (Brazil, Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay)
  • NAFTA/USMCA — free trade agreement between US, Canada, and Mexico
  • IMF (International Monetary Fund) — provides emergency loans to countries facing financial crises; often attaches conditions requiring economic reforms
  • World Bank — provides development loans to poorer countries for infrastructure, education, health projects
International Debt: Many developing countries borrowed heavily from the IMF and World Bank. Structural adjustment programs (conditions attached to loans) often required cutting social spending, leading to controversy about whether they help or harm development.

7.7 Changes as a Result of the World Economy
▼

Fordism vs. Post-Fordism

  • Fordism — mass production using assembly lines (pioneered by Henry Ford); standardized products; workers do one repetitive task; large factories
  • Post-Fordism — flexible production; smaller batches; varied products; workers multi-task; responds quickly to market demand; relies on technology
  • Transition from Fordism to Post-Fordism = shift in how and where things are made

Offshoring vs. Outsourcing

  • Offshoring — moving production operations to another country (to access cheaper labor or resources)
  • Outsourcing — contracting specific tasks or services to outside companies (domestic or foreign)
  • Both reduce costs for companies but can eliminate jobs in home country
  • Example: US companies outsource call centers to India; offshore manufacturing to China

Deindustrialization

  • Decline of manufacturing in developed countries as factories move overseas
  • Rust Belt in US: former manufacturing cities (Detroit, Pittsburgh) lost jobs
  • Workers shift toward service and knowledge-based jobs
  • Creates economic hardship in affected regions

New International Division of Labor

  • Manufacturing has shifted to developing countries with lower labor costs
  • Developed countries focus on R&D, design, management (quaternary/quinary)
  • Global supply chains: components made in multiple countries, assembled elsewhere
  • Example: iPhone designed in US, components from many countries, assembled in China

Special Economic Zones (SEZs) & EPZs

  • SEZ — areas within a country with special economic regulations to attract foreign investment (lower taxes, relaxed regulations)
  • Export-Processing Zones (EPZs) — areas where goods are manufactured primarily for export with reduced tariffs and labor regulations
  • Maquiladoras — factories along US-Mexico border; US companies set up manufacturing in Mexico for cheaper labor; goods exported to US
  • China's SEZs (e.g., Shenzhen) — key to China's rapid industrialization

Economic Geography Concepts

  • Multiplier effect — one new industry attracts others; creates jobs; stimulates whole regional economy
  • Agglomeration — clustering of related businesses/industries to share infrastructure, labor, and knowledge
  • Economies of scale — cost advantages that arise from larger production volumes (per-unit cost decreases)
  • Just-in-time delivery — inventory arrives exactly when needed; reduces storage costs; requires reliable supply chains
  • Growth pole — a leading sector or region that stimulates economic activity in surrounding areas

High Technology Industries & Silicon Valley

  • High-tech industries require skilled labor, R&D investment, and university partnerships
  • Silicon Valley (California) — global center of tech industry; grew from Stanford University research, venture capital, and clustering of tech firms
  • Research Triangle (North Carolina) — grew around Duke, UNC, NC State; government investment was crucial
  • High-tech clusters create massive multiplier effects in surrounding regions

7.8 Sustainable Development
▼

What is Sustainable Development?

Development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs. (UN Brundtland Commission, 1987)

  • Balances economic growth, social equity, and environmental protection
  • Recognized that unlimited growth on a finite planet is impossible
  • UN adopted 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) in 2015

Resource Depletion

  • Overuse of natural resources at rates faster than they can be replenished
  • Includes: deforestation, overfishing, groundwater depletion, fossil fuel exhaustion
  • Sustainable development seeks to use resources at sustainable rates
  • Renewable vs. non-renewable resources: key distinction for sustainability

Environmental Pollution

  • Point source pollution — pollution from a single, identifiable source (factory pipe, oil spill)
  • Nonpoint source pollution — pollution from diffuse sources (agricultural runoff, urban stormwater)
  • Sustainable dev. addresses both through regulations, clean technology, and better practices

Climate Change

  • Driven by greenhouse gas emissions from burning fossil fuels
  • Causes: rising sea levels, extreme weather, habitat destruction
  • Carbon neutrality — achieving net zero carbon emissions
  • Cogeneration — generating electricity from waste heat; increases efficiency
  • International agreements: Paris Agreement (2015)

Ecotourism

  • Environmentally responsible travel to natural areas that conserves the environment and improves local well-being
  • Provides economic incentive to preserve natural habitats
  • Local communities benefit directly from conservation
  • Examples: wildlife safaris in Kenya, rainforest tours in Costa Rica

UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)

  • 17 goals adopted by all UN member states in 2015 as part of the 2030 Agenda
  • Replaced the Millennium Development Goals (MDGs)
  • Cover: poverty, hunger, health, education, gender equality, clean water, affordable energy, decent work, innovation, reduced inequality, sustainable cities, responsible consumption, climate action, life below water, life on land, peace & justice, partnerships
  • Goal: transform the world by addressing the most pressing economic, social, and environmental challenges
Sustainable Development addresses: resource depletion → manage resources responsibly; mass consumption → reduce, reuse, recycle; pollution → clean technology, regulations; climate change → carbon neutrality, renewable energy; human issues → health, equity, education for all.
